How to Measure

Two measurements are all this calculator needs, both taken with a soft measuring tape and no padding.

  • Underbust (band) — wrap the tape directly under your bust, snug and level, following the same line all the way around your ribcage. It should feel firm but not tight enough to restrict breathing.
  • Bust — wrap the tape loosely around the fullest part of your bust, keeping it parallel to the floor. Wearing a well-fitting (non-padded) bra while measuring bust gives a more consistent reading than measuring bare.

Round both measurements to the nearest whole inch or half-centimeter. Studies and industry surveys are frequently cited suggesting a large share of women wear an incorrectly sized bra — usually a band that's too big and a cup that's too small, which is why a genuine measurement-based starting point matters more than guessing from a label.

How the Calculation Works

This calculator uses the traditional US sizing method, still the most widely referenced starting point across fitting guides. The band size comes from your underbust measurement, rounded to an even number, plus 4 inches. The cup size comes from the difference between your bust and underbust measurements — each inch of difference steps up one cup letter (0" = AA, 1" = A, 2" = B, 3" = C, 4" = D, and so on).

It's worth knowing this traditional method isn't universally agreed upon — some modern fit specialists argue for methods that add less (or nothing) to the underbust measurement, on the reasoning that band construction and elastic have changed significantly since the traditional formula was popularized decades ago. Because of this, and because manufacturing varies by brand, treat any calculator's output — including this one — as an evidence-based starting point rather than a guaranteed fit.

A Guide to Breast Shapes

Cup letter alone doesn't capture how tissue is distributed, which is why the same letter can fit very differently depending on shape. Here's what each profile in the tool above generally means, and the mechanical fitting challenge each one tends to create:

Round

Similar fullness above and below the nipple line. Round shapes are usually the most forgiving to fit, since volume is evenly distributed and most cup constructions accommodate them well.

Teardrop

Less fullness on top, more on the bottom — the classic sloped profile. A plunge or balconette cut, which is cut lower at the top of the cup, tends to follow this natural slope better than a full, rounded cup shape.

East-West

Breast tissue points outward toward the sides rather than straight forward. Bras with side support panels or "wings" help center tissue forward, reducing both side spillage and gapping at the center.

Side Set

A wider gap between the breasts, with tissue positioned toward the outside of the chest. A narrower center gore (the panel between the cups) and side support help bring the profile together visually and physically.

Slender

Less overall volume relative to chest size, often with a flatter profile at the top of the cup. Lightly padded or push-up styles can add shape without the bulk of a heavily structured full-coverage cup.

Relaxed / Pendant

Breast tissue sits lower on the chest wall with less firmness at the top — a natural result of tissue composition and gravity over time, not something "wrong" with the body. Full-coverage styles with a wide band and supportive straps generally give the most comfortable, secure fit for this profile.

What Are Sister Sizes?

A sister size is a different band-and-cup combination that holds roughly the same cup volume as your calculated size — useful when a size is close but not quite right. Moving to a sister size with a looser band and smaller-lettered cup (for example, 34C → 36B) keeps the same volume in a bigger, more relaxed band. Moving to a tighter band and bigger-lettered cup (34C → 32D) keeps the same volume in a smaller, firmer band. This is the mechanism behind most "if it's not quite right, try this instead" fitting advice — the cup letter isn't a fixed volume, it only means something relative to its band number.

Signs Your Bra Doesn't Fit

  • Spilling — breast tissue overflowing above or around the cup usually means the cup is too small, not that the band needs to change; try the same band with a bigger cup letter.
  • Gapping — empty, unfilled space in the cup usually means the cup is too big for that band; try a smaller cup letter or a differently-shaped cup (like a plunge instead of a full cup).
  • Riding up the back — the band is usually too big; a properly fitting band stays level and doesn't creep upward during the day.
  • Digging straps — since a properly fitting band should provide most of the support, straps digging in often signals the band is too loose, forcing the straps to compensate.
  • Underwire poking or resting on breast tissue — the wire should sit flat against the ribcage at the sides and center, not on tissue itself; this usually means the cup size or shape is wrong for your profile.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does breast shape affect my final size?

The number and letter from the formula stay the same regardless of shape, but shape determines which cup *construction* (plunge, balconette, full-coverage, and so on) will actually contain that volume comfortably — this is why two people with the same calculated size can need very different bra styles.

What's the best bra style for a relaxed or pendant shape?

Full-coverage styles with a wide band, supportive (often wider-set) straps, and a cup cut lower at the top tend to work best, since they follow where the tissue naturally sits rather than trying to lift it into a shape it doesn't hold.

Can a sister size fix gapping or spilling?

Often, yes — if a size gaps, the tighter-band/bigger-cup sister size keeps the same band snugness while giving slightly more room in the cup's shape without changing the underlying volume; if a size spills only slightly, the looser-band/smaller-cup sister size can sometimes resolve it without losing support.

Why do my measurements give a size that doesn't match what I usually buy?

This is extremely common — sizing isn't standardized across brands, and vanity sizing (labeling garments smaller than their actual measurements) is widespread. Use this result as a starting point for trying on, not a guarantee of the exact label you'll need in every brand.

Should I measure over or under clothing?

Under light clothing or directly on skin gives the most accurate reading — avoid measuring over thick sweaters or padded bras, which can add an inch or more to both measurements and skew the result.
