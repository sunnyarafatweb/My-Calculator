Why Four Modes Instead of One

Percentage questions show up in different shapes depending on what you already know and what you're solving for. All four modes above use the exact same underlying relationship — part = percent × whole — just rearranged to isolate a different piece. Rather than making you do algebra to rearrange the formula yourself, each tab is already solved for the variable you're missing.

The Four Question Shapes, Explained

X% of Y

The most common shape: you know a percentage and a total, and want the piece. Tipping 20% on a $45 bill, or finding what 8% sales tax adds to a purchase, are both this shape.

X is what % of Y

You know two actual amounts and want to express one as a percentage of the other — like figuring out what percentage of your monthly income rent takes up.

X is Y% of what

You know a piece and the percentage it represents, and want the original total — useful when you know a discount amount and the discount rate, and want to reconstruct the original price.

Percent Change

You have two values at different points — a before and after — and want to know the percentage swing between them. Works for both increases and decreases; the calculator labels the direction automatically.

A Quick Sanity Check

All four modes are internally consistent with each other. If 25% of 80 is 20, then 20 must also be exactly 25% of 80, and 20 must be 25% of 80's "what number." Try flipping between tabs with related numbers to see the relationship hold — it's a good way to build intuition for percentages generally.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between percentage change and "X% of Y"?

Percentage change compares two different values to each other (how much did something grow or shrink), while "X% of Y" calculates a portion of a single value — genuinely different questions that just both involve percentages.

Why is my percent change negative?

A negative result means the second number is smaller than the first — a decrease. The calculator shows the direction in words too ("increase" or "decrease") so the sign is never ambiguous.

Can I use negative numbers?

Yes for the "to" value in percent change (representing a value that went negative), but percentages of negative totals rarely make practical sense — double-check your inputs if a result looks unexpected.

How is percentage point different from percentage?

If a rate moves from 5% to 8%, that's a 3 percentage point increase but a 60% relative increase (3 divided by 5). This calculator's "% Change" mode gives the relative percentage, not percentage points — a common source of confusion in news headlines about rates.
