Balance & Interest Over Time

A mortgage calculator takes the guesswork out of one of the largest financial commitments most people ever make. By entering your home price, down payment, interest rate, and loan term, you get an immediate picture of what your monthly obligation will look like — and how much of your hard-earned money will go toward interest versus actually owning more of your home. This calculator is built for U.S. residents and reflects standard fixed-rate mortgage math, though the principles apply broadly.

How Mortgages Actually Work

When you borrow money to buy a home, you're entering a long-term agreement to repay the lender — with interest — over a fixed period. Each monthly payment covers two things: a portion of the original loan amount (the principal) and the cost of borrowing that money (the interest). In the early years of a mortgage, the vast majority of your payment is interest. It's only as years pass that the split begins shifting meaningfully toward principal.

This gradual payoff process is called amortization. It's why the amortization schedule in this calculator is so revealing: you can watch, year by year, how your balance slowly decreases and how the interest-to-principal ratio changes. If you've ever felt like you've been paying your mortgage faithfully and yet your balance barely moves in the first few years — amortization is why. The math isn't unfair; it's just a reflection of interest being charged on a large outstanding balance.

What Goes Into a Monthly Mortgage Payment

Most homeowners don't just pay principal and interest each month. A realistic mortgage payment typically bundles several other costs through an escrow account — meaning your lender collects these amounts monthly and pays them on your behalf when they come due.

Property Taxes

Local and county governments levy annual property taxes based on the assessed value of your home. In the U.S., the average property tax rate hovers around 1% of home value per year, but rates vary dramatically by state and county. New Jersey homeowners, for example, pay some of the highest effective rates in the country, while some Southern states have rates well below the national average. Your lender will typically include one-twelfth of your annual tax bill in each monthly payment.

Homeowners Insurance

Also called hazard insurance, this policy protects against damage to your home from events like fire, windstorms, and certain other perils. Lenders require this coverage to protect their collateral. Annual premiums average around $1,500 nationwide, though coastal properties prone to hurricanes or homes in high-risk wildfire zones can cost several times that amount.

Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI)

If your down payment is less than 20% of the purchase price, most conventional lenders will require PMI. This insurance protects the lender — not you — in case you stop making payments. PMI typically costs between 0.5% and 1.5% of the original loan amount per year. It's worth noting that PMI is not permanent: once your loan balance drops to 80% of the home's value, you can request cancellation. Federal law requires lenders to automatically cancel it when you reach 78%.

HOA Fees

If your property is part of a homeowners association — common in condos, townhome communities, and planned developments — you'll owe monthly or annual dues. These vary enormously, from under $100 a month in some neighborhoods to several hundred dollars in premium developments. HOA fees cover shared amenities and common-area maintenance but are not included in your mortgage payment; you pay them separately.

Mortgage Calculator Components Explained

Loan Amount

The amount you borrow is the purchase price minus your down payment. A larger down payment reduces your loan amount, lowers your monthly payment, eliminates PMI if you reach 20%, and saves you a substantial amount of interest over the loan's life. The maximum loan you can qualify for is driven by your income, debt-to-income ratio, and credit score — your lender will assess all of these during underwriting.

Down Payment

The conventional advice of 20% down exists for good reason: it eliminates PMI and signals to lenders that you're a lower-risk borrower. But it's not a hard requirement. FHA loans allow as little as 3.5% down. VA loans and USDA loans for eligible borrowers can require zero down payment. The tradeoff is always the same: a smaller down payment means a larger loan, higher monthly payments, more interest paid over time, and often PMI costs layered on top.

Loan Term

Most U.S. homebuyers choose between a 15-year and 30-year fixed-rate mortgage. The 30-year option dominates because it offers lower monthly payments, but you'll pay considerably more in total interest over three decades compared to a 15-year loan. Shorter terms come with lower interest rates and accelerate equity building, but the higher monthly payment requires a more stable income to sustain comfortably.

Interest Rate

Your mortgage interest rate is the annual cost of borrowing expressed as a percentage. Fixed-rate mortgages lock in your rate for the life of the loan, giving you predictable payments. Adjustable-rate mortgages (ARMs) start with a fixed period (often 5 or 7 years) and then adjust periodically based on a benchmark index. ARMs can be advantageous if you plan to sell before the adjustment period, but they carry risk if rates rise significantly. The rate you qualify for depends on your credit score, down payment, loan type, and broader market conditions.

Costs Associated With Buying a Home

The monthly payment is only part of the financial picture. Buying a home involves upfront and ongoing costs that can catch first-time buyers off guard.

Recurring Costs

Beyond the mortgage payment itself, homeowners routinely pay for utilities, lawn care or landscaping, minor repairs, and general maintenance. Financial advisors often suggest budgeting 1% of the home's value annually for maintenance — for a $400,000 home, that's $4,000 a year, or roughly $333 per month. Older homes may require more. These aren't optional costs; deferred maintenance compounds quickly and often leads to more expensive repairs later.

Property taxes tend to rise over time, particularly in areas with growing demand or escalating home values. Many homeowners see their escrow payment increase annually as tax assessments are updated. Factoring in a modest annual increase to your projections — the "Annual Tax & Cost Increase" fields in the calculator above — gives you a more realistic long-term view.

Non-Recurring Costs

Closing costs are the fees paid at settlement when the home purchase is finalized. In the U.S., buyers typically pay 2% to 5% of the purchase price in closing costs. On a $400,000 home, that can mean $8,000 to $20,000 due at closing. These costs include lender origination fees, title insurance, title search fees, appraisal fees, attorney fees in some states, prepaid homeowners insurance, prepaid property taxes, and prepaid mortgage interest.

Moving costs are another immediate expense that varies widely — a local move might run a few hundred dollars with rented equipment, while a cross-country relocation can easily cost several thousand dollars when hiring professionals.

Initial home improvements are easy to underestimate before you move in. Fresh paint, new flooring, updated appliances, window treatments, and basic landscaping are expenses many buyers face in the first year. It's prudent to maintain a cash reserve beyond your down payment and closing costs specifically for these early-ownership expenses.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much house can I afford?

A widely used guideline is that your total housing costs (mortgage payment, taxes, insurance) shouldn't exceed 28% of your gross monthly income, and your total debt payments shouldn't exceed 36–43%. Lenders have their own debt-to-income requirements, but keeping housing costs below 25–30% of take-home pay gives you breathing room for savings and unexpected expenses.

Is it worth making extra mortgage payments?

Extra principal payments reduce your loan balance faster, which means you pay interest on a smaller amount each subsequent month. Even an additional $100–$200 per month can shave years off a 30-year mortgage and save thousands in interest. Use the 'More Options' section in the calculator above to see exactly how extra payments affect your payoff date and total interest.

When does PMI go away?

Under the Homeowners Protection Act, lenders must automatically cancel PMI when your loan balance reaches 78% of the original purchase price, based on your scheduled payment date. You can also request cancellation at 80% if your home hasn't declined in value. Some lenders require a formal appraisal to confirm value. Paying down your principal faster than scheduled can help you reach the 80% threshold sooner.

What is the difference between interest rate and APR?

The interest rate is the cost of borrowing the principal only. The Annual Percentage Rate (APR) is a broader measure that includes the interest rate plus certain fees — origination fees, mortgage points, and other costs — expressed as an annual percentage. The APR gives you a more complete picture of the true cost of the loan and is the better number to use when comparing loan offers from different lenders.
