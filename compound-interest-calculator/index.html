The Formula Behind Compounding

For a lump sum with regular contributions, each compounding period the balance grows by the periodic rate and then gets the new contribution added on top — so future contributions never miss out on growth already earned, and past growth immediately starts earning its own return. Written out for one lump sum with no further deposits, it's simply:

Future Value = Principal × (1 + r/n)n×t

where r is the annual rate, n is how many times per year it compounds, and t is the number of years. Add monthly contributions into that same loop and you get exactly what the calculator above computes — principal and every future deposit compounding together.

Why the Contribution Habit Matters More Than the Rate

Over short time horizons, what you contribute dominates the total. Over long ones, growth on growth starts to take over — which is why starting early tends to matter more than chasing a slightly higher return. This calculator separates the two so you can see exactly how much of your final balance is money you actually put in.

A Worked Example

Starting with $10,000 and adding $200 a month at a 7% annual return, compounded monthly, grows to roughly $144,573 after 20 years. Of that, only $58,000 was actually contributed — the rest, over $86,500, is pure growth. Extend the timeline to 30 years and the balance climbs well past $300,000, with growth making up the clear majority of it.

The Rule of 72: A Fast Mental Shortcut

Want a quick estimate without opening a calculator at all? Divide 72 by your annual rate to get roughly how many years it takes a lump sum to double. At 6%, that's 12 years; at 9%, about 8 years. It's not exact — the real math involves logarithms — but it's accurate enough for a gut-check, and it's the fastest way to compare two rates in your head.

Compounding Frequency: How Much Does It Really Change?

More frequent compounding always helps a little, but the gap shrinks the more often it already happens. Moving from annual to monthly compounding makes a real difference; moving from monthly to daily barely moves the needle further, since the effective annual rate is already close to its ceiling. The rate itself, and how long you leave the money invested, matter far more than squeezing out the last bit of compounding frequency.

Frequently Asked Questions

What return rate should I use?

For a savings account, use the actual advertised APY. For investments, there's no guaranteed number — long-run stock market averages are often cited in the 6–10% range before inflation, but any individual year can be sharply higher or lower. Try a few different rates to see a realistic range of outcomes.

Why does the growth line curve upward instead of staying straight?

That curve is compounding in action — early growth is mostly driven by your contributions, but as the balance grows, the interest earned on previous interest becomes a bigger share of each year's growth. This effect accelerates the longer the money stays invested.

Does this account for taxes or inflation?

No — this shows nominal growth before taxes on gains (if any apply in a taxable account) and before inflation erodes purchasing power. Both can meaningfully change the real-world value of the future amount shown.

Is compound interest the same as APY?

APY (Annual Percentage Yield) is simply the effective annual rate after a given compounding frequency has already been applied — it's the output of the compounding formula, not a separate concept. A 6% rate compounded monthly has an APY of about 6.17%.

What if I stop contributing partway through?

Whatever balance you've built keeps compounding on its own — you'll just lose the extra boost that new contributions would have added going forward. Try setting the monthly contribution to $0 partway through your own planning to see how much a lump sum alone would grow.
