Understanding dividend yield

Dividend yield expresses a company's annual dividend payment as a percentage of its current share price, making it possible to compare income potential across stocks of very different prices. A $150 stock paying $6 a year and a $15 stock paying $0.60 a year both yield exactly 4% — the dollar amounts differ, but the income relative to the investment is identical.

Dividend Yield = (Annual Dividend Per Share ÷ Current Stock Price) × 100

If you only know the per-payment amount, annualize it first by multiplying by how many times it's paid each year — quarterly payments ×4, monthly ×12, semi-annual ×2. In the default example on this page — a $150 stock paying $1.50 every quarter — the annualized dividend is $6.00, giving a 4.0% yield. Skipping this step and dividing the per-payment amount directly by price is one of the most common calculation mistakes.

Forward yield vs. trailing yield

Trailing (or TTM, "trailing twelve months") yield sums the actual dividends paid over the past year and divides by the current price — it's grounded in confirmed, real payments but can lag behind a recent change. Forward yield instead annualizes the most recently declared dividend, which is more current if a company just raised or cut its payout, but assumes that new rate holds for the full year ahead. Most brokerage apps display trailing yield by default; check a company's latest dividend announcement if you want the forward-looking figure.

Yield on cost: your personal return

Yield on cost divides the current annual dividend by what you originally paid for the shares, not today's market price. Someone who bought at $50 a share and now collects $4 a year in dividends has an 8% yield on cost, even though a new buyer today — paying, say, $100 a share for the same stock — only receives a 4% yield. Yield on cost naturally rises over time for long-term holders in companies that consistently grow their dividend, since the payment increases while the original purchase price never changes.

Yield on Cost = (Current Annual Dividend ÷ Original Purchase Price) × 100

Why a very high yield can be a warning, not a bargain

Because yield is a ratio, it automatically rises whenever the stock price falls — even with no change to the dividend itself. A company paying a steady $3 a year yields 3% at a $100 share price, but if bad news drives the price down to $40, the same unchanged dividend suddenly yields 7.5%. That inflated number often precedes a dividend cut, as the company reduces the payment to preserve cash — after a cut to $1.20 a year at the new $40 price, the yield falls right back to a more ordinary 3%. This pattern is commonly called a "yield trap": an eye-catching yield produced by distress rather than opportunity. Checking a company's payout ratio, free cash flow, and dividend history helps separate a genuinely attractive yield from one that's about to disappear.

Projecting yield on cost with dividend growth

If a company has a track record of raising its dividend by a fairly consistent percentage each year, that growth rate can be projected forward to estimate how yield on cost might climb over time. A stock bought at $50 with a current $2 annual dividend growing at 7% a year would be paying roughly $3.93 a year after 10 years, for a projected yield on cost near 7.9% — nearly double the starting yield, purely from dividend growth compounding on a fixed purchase price. This is a projection based on past growth continuing, not a guarantee.

DRIP: reinvesting instead of taking cash

A Dividend Reinvestment Plan automatically uses cash dividends to buy additional shares of the same stock instead of paying them out. Those additional shares then earn their own dividends and participate in any price appreciation, compounding the position over time in a way that simply collecting the dividend as cash does not. Whether reinvesting or taking cash is better depends on individual goals — DRIP tends to build a larger position faster, while cash dividends provide spendable income along the way.

Glossary of terms

Dividend yield
The annual dividend per share expressed as a percentage of the current share price.
Trailing (TTM) yield
Yield calculated using the actual dividends paid over the past 12 months.
Forward yield
Yield calculated by annualizing the most recently declared dividend payment.
Yield on cost (YOC)
The current annual dividend divided by an investor's original purchase price, rather than today's price.
DRIP (Dividend Reinvestment Plan)
A program that automatically uses cash dividends to purchase additional shares instead of paying them out.
Yield trap
A misleadingly high yield caused by a falling share price, often preceding a dividend cut.
Payout ratio
The percentage of a company's earnings paid out as dividends — a key indicator of how sustainable a dividend is.
Special dividend
A one-time dividend payment tied to an unusual event, typically excluded from regular yield calculations.

Common mistakes & tips for evaluating dividend yield

  • Forgetting to annualize the dividend. Dividing a quarterly or monthly payment directly by price, without multiplying by the payment frequency first, dramatically understates the true yield.
  • Chasing the highest yield without checking sustainability. A very high yield is frequently a symptom of a falling price and possible upcoming dividend cut, not a bargain.
  • Including special dividends in ongoing yield estimates. One-time payouts inflate the calculation and don't reflect repeatable income.
  • Confusing dividend rate with dividend yield. The rate is a dollar amount per share; the yield is that amount expressed as a percentage of price — they answer different questions.
  • Ignoring yield on cost for long-held positions. Current yield reflects what a new buyer gets today, not the often much higher return a long-term holder is actually receiving.
  • Assuming dividend growth will continue indefinitely. Past dividend increases don't guarantee future ones — a growth-rate projection is an estimate, not a promise.

Healthy yield vs. yield trap — the same dividend, different stories

A company paying a steady $3-a-year dividend, before and after a sharp price decline.

ScenarioPriceDividendYield
Healthy$100$3.00/yr3.00%
Yield trap (price crashed, dividend not yet cut)$40$3.00/yr7.50%
After the inevitable dividend cut$40$1.20/yr3.00%

The 7.50% figure in the middle row looks like a great deal, but it exists only because the price collapsed while the dividend hadn't caught up yet — the company's underlying ability to keep paying $3 a year likely hasn't improved, and often has gotten worse. Once the dividend is cut to a level the company can actually sustain, the yield falls back to something much more ordinary. The lesson: a yield spike caused by a falling price deserves scrutiny of the business, not automatic enthusiasm about the bigger number.
