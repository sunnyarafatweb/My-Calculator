Understanding compound interest

Compound interest is interest calculated on both the money you originally put in and the interest that money has already earned. Over short time horizons, what you contribute dominates the total balance. Over long horizons, growth on growth starts to take over — which is why starting early tends to matter more than chasing a slightly higher return.

FV = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t) + PMT × [((1 + r/n)^(n×t) − 1) / (r/n)]

Where P is your initial amount, r is the annual return rate, n is the compounding frequency per year, t is the number of years, and PMT is your periodic contribution. This calculator runs that formula behind every tab above — solving for the future value directly, or working backwards to find the contribution, rate, or time you'd need to hit a target.

Why the contribution habit matters more than the rate

Early on, your balance grows mainly because you keep adding to it. As the account matures, an increasing share of each year's growth comes from interest earned on interest, not new deposits. That's the "curve" you see in the growth chart above bending upward over time rather than climbing in a straight line.

Compounding frequency: annually, monthly, daily

Compounding frequency controls how often earned interest is added to your balance so it starts earning its own return. Daily compounding credits interest most often and produces a very slightly higher balance than monthly, which in turn edges out annual compounding — the gap is usually small unless the rate is high or the time horizon is long, but it's real, and this calculator lets you toggle between all three to see the exact difference for your numbers.

Nominal vs. real (inflation-adjusted) returns

The headline future value above is nominal — it doesn't subtract taxes on gains or account for inflation eroding purchasing power. The milestone stats section includes a real value estimate using a default 3% inflation assumption, so you can see roughly what that future balance would be worth in today's money.

Compound interest vs. simple interest

Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal, so it grows in a straight line. Compound interest is calculated on principal plus all previously earned interest, so it grows along a curve that steepens over time. The longer the time horizon and the higher the rate, the bigger the gap between the two becomes.

A worked example

Starting with a $10,000 lump sum and adding $200 a month at a 7% annual return, compounded monthly, grows to roughly $144,600 after 20 years. Of that, only about $58,000 was actually contributed — the rest, close to $86,600, is growth. Stretch the same scenario to 30 years and the balance climbs past $325,000, with growth making up roughly three-quarters of the total. Try adjusting the inputs above to see how your own numbers play out.

Effective annual rate and continuous compounding

When interest compounds more often than once a year, the effective annual rate (EAR) — the actual annual growth rate once compounding is accounted for — runs slightly higher than the stated nominal rate. The formula is EAR = (1 + r/n)^n − 1. Taken to its limit, compounding "continuously" (n → ∞) gives EAR = e^r − 1, the theoretical ceiling for how much a given nominal rate can produce — daily compounding already gets extremely close to this limit for typical rates.

Glossary of terms

Principal
The initial lump sum you start with, before any contributions or interest are added.
Contribution (PMT)
The regular amount you add on top of the principal — modeled here as a fixed monthly deposit.
Future Value (FV)
The total balance your principal and contributions grow to after compounding over the chosen time horizon.
Compounding period
How often interest is calculated and added to the balance — annually, monthly, or daily in this calculator.
APY vs. APR
APR is the stated nominal annual rate; APY (annual percentage yield) already includes the effect of compounding, so APY is always equal to or higher than APR for the same account.
Rule of 72
A shortcut estimate for years-to-double: divide 72 by the annual interest rate.
Nominal vs. real return
Nominal is the raw percentage growth; real return subtracts inflation to show growth in actual purchasing power.
Present Value (PV)
The value today of an amount you'll receive in the future, found by discounting it back at your assumed rate — the reverse of future value.

Common mistakes & tips to maximize growth

  • Waiting to start. Because growth compounds on itself, a 10-year head start is often worth more than a much larger contribution begun a decade later — try comparing the two in the Future Value tab.
  • Assuming a fixed rate is guaranteed. Historical averages are not promises — real returns vary a lot year to year, and sequence-of-returns risk matters most right before you need the money.
  • Ignoring fees. Ongoing account or fund fees quietly reduce your effective annual return every year — a 1% fee on a 7% return is a meaningful bite out of your growth over decades.
  • Withdrawing early. Pulling money out interrupts compounding and can trigger penalties or lost tax advantages, especially in retirement accounts.
  • Forgetting inflation. A future balance that sounds large today may buy less in real terms — check the real (inflation-adjusted) value estimate in the milestones section.
  • Not automating contributions. Automatic monthly transfers remove the temptation to skip a month and keep the compounding schedule consistent.
