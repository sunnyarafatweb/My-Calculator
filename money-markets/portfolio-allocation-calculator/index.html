Understanding portfolio allocation and rebalancing

Asset allocation is how your money is split across categories like stocks, bonds, real estate and cash. Your target allocation reflects the level of risk you've chosen to take on; your current allocation is whatever it has drifted to, since different assets grow at different rates over time without any action on your part.

Target Amount = Total Portfolio × Target % ÷ 100
Rebalance Amount = Target Amount − Current Amount

A positive rebalance amount means buying more of that asset; a negative amount means selling some of it. In the default example on this page — a $100,000 portfolio targeting 40% US stocks, 20% international stocks, 25% bonds, 10% real estate and 5% cash — US stocks have drifted to 45% of the portfolio while international stocks have fallen to 15%, calling for a $5,000 sale of US stocks and a $5,000 purchase of international stocks to return to target.

Why portfolios drift on their own

A portfolio that starts at a 60% stock / 40% bond split doesn't stay there without effort. In a simple five-year example where stocks average 10% annual returns and bonds average 4%, an unrebalanced 60/40 portfolio drifts to roughly 66.5% stocks and 33.5% bonds — a meaningfully more aggressive mix than originally intended, arrived at without a single deliberate decision. This is the core reason rebalancing matters: left alone, a portfolio's risk level tends to drift upward during strong stock markets, right when a correction becomes more likely.

Threshold-based vs. calendar-based rebalancing

Calendar-based rebalancing checks and corrects the portfolio on a fixed schedule, commonly annually. Threshold-based rebalancing instead waits until an asset drifts beyond a chosen band — often 5 percentage points — before acting, regardless of how much time has passed. Both are reasonable approaches; threshold-based rebalancing tends to react faster to large, sudden moves, while calendar-based rebalancing is simpler to stick to and generates fewer, more predictable trades.

Starting points: age-based and risk-profile models

The "100 minus age" rule is a simple starting point for a stock/bond split — subtract your age from 100 for a suggested stock percentage, with the rest in bonds. Because lifespans have lengthened and retirements last longer, many planners now use 110 or 120 minus age for a more growth-oriented mix, particularly for money that won't be needed for decades. Separately, common reference risk-profile models run roughly 30% stocks / 60% bonds / 10% cash for a conservative investor, 60% / 35% / 5% for moderate, and 85% / 10% / 5% for aggressive. All of these are illustrative starting points to adjust from, not a personalized recommendation — actual circumstances, income stability, and other assets all matter too.

Rebalancing with new contributions instead of selling

Selling an appreciated asset in a taxable account can trigger capital gains tax, even when the sale is purely to restore a target allocation. Contribution-first rebalancing avoids this by directing new money toward whichever assets are currently underweight, moving the portfolio closer to target without selling anything. If the new contribution isn't large enough to fully close every gap, it's distributed across the underweight assets proportional to how far each has drifted, and any remaining gap is reported so you know how much selling (if any) would still be needed for a complete rebalance.

A worked example

Same $100,000 portfolio as above, with a $3,000 new contribution instead of selling. At the new $103,000 total, international stocks, bonds, real estate and cash are all underweight by a combined $6,800; the $3,000 contribution covers about 44% of that gap, split proportionally — roughly $2,471 to international stocks, $331 to bonds, $132 to real estate, and $66 to cash. US stocks remain overweight by about $3,800, which would still need a sale to fully close, but the contribution alone moved the portfolio meaningfully closer to target without touching existing holdings.

Rebalancing isn't free

Every trade can carry transaction costs, and selling appreciated positions in taxable accounts can trigger real tax bills. Rebalancing too frequently on tiny drifts adds cost without much benefit; a reasonable threshold band or an annual schedule usually captures most of the benefit of rebalancing without excessive trading.

Glossary of terms

Asset allocation
How a portfolio is divided across asset categories such as stocks, bonds, real estate and cash, usually expressed as percentages.
Target allocation
The percentage split an investor intends to hold in each asset category, based on risk tolerance and time horizon.
Portfolio drift
The gradual divergence of actual allocation from target allocation as different assets grow at different rates.
Rebalancing
Buying or selling assets to bring a portfolio's actual allocation back in line with its target allocation.
Threshold (rebalancing band)
A tolerance range around a target allocation; a trade is only triggered once drift exceeds this band.
Contribution-first rebalancing
Directing new money toward underweight assets instead of selling overweight ones, often to avoid triggering capital gains tax.
"100 minus age" rule
A rule of thumb suggesting a stock allocation equal to 100 minus your age, with the remainder in bonds.
Turnover
The total dollar value of buy and sell trades needed to complete a rebalance, a rough indicator of how far a portfolio had drifted.

Common mistakes & tips for portfolio allocation

  • Never checking allocation after setting it once. A portfolio left completely alone drifts further from target every year markets move unevenly.
  • Rebalancing too frequently. Trading on every tiny drift adds costs and, in taxable accounts, taxes, without meaningfully reducing risk.
  • Selling in taxable accounts when a contribution would do. Contribution-first rebalancing often avoids triggering capital gains entirely.
  • Setting targets that don't sum to 100%. A target allocation that doesn't add up leaves part of the portfolio without a defined destination.
  • Treating a rule of thumb as a personalized plan. Age-based and risk-profile models are starting points \u2014 income stability, other assets, and goals all matter too.
  • Ignoring tax-advantaged accounts as a rebalancing tool. Trades inside a 401(k) or IRA don't trigger capital gains, making them a lower-cost place to do the bulk of a rebalance.
