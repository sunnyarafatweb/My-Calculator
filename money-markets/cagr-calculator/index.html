Understanding CAGR

CAGR — the Compound Annual Growth Rate — is the single constant annual rate that would carry a beginning value to an ending value over a given number of years, as if growth had happened smoothly every year instead of in real, uneven jumps. It's the standard way to compare the performance of stocks, funds, businesses or any other value that changes over time, because it collapses a messy multi-year history into one comparable number.

CAGR = (Ending Value ÷ Beginning Value)^(1 ÷ Years) − 1

Where Ending Value and Beginning Value are the two data points you're measuring between, and Years is the length of that period. This calculator runs that formula directly on the "Find CAGR" tab, and rearranges it algebraically on the other tabs to solve for the ending value, the starting amount, or the number of years instead.

Why CAGR differs from the average annual return

The arithmetic average simply adds up each year's percentage return and divides by the number of years. That number can be badly misleading whenever returns are volatile, because percentage losses and gains aren't symmetric — losing 50% and then gaining 50% back doesn't return you to even, it leaves you down 25%. CAGR avoids this trap because it's a geometric measure based on the actual beginning and ending value, not an average of percentages.

Take a concrete example: a $10,000 position rises 50% in year one to $15,000, then falls 33.33% in year two back down to exactly $10,000. The arithmetic average of +50% and −33.33% is +8.33% — a number that suggests healthy growth. But nothing was actually gained; the position is back where it started. The CAGR correctly reports 0.00%, because that's what really happened to the money. See the side-by-side table further down the page for the full breakdown.

CAGR vs. IRR and XIRR

CAGR assumes one lump sum invested at the start and nothing added or withdrawn until the end. If you invested in installments — monthly contributions, several separate purchases, or partial withdrawals — CAGR on the raw start and end values won't accurately reflect your personal return, because it ignores when each dollar was actually put to work. IRR, and its irregular-dates version XIRR, are built for exactly that case: they solve for the rate that makes the timing and size of every cash flow consistent with the final value. Use CAGR for a clean two-point comparison; use XIRR when money moved in or out along the way.

Common uses of CAGR

  • Investment performance. Comparing a stock, ETF, mutual fund or crypto position's growth over a chosen holding period.
  • Business metrics. Revenue growth, user or subscriber growth, and other company KPIs are routinely reported as a CAGR over 3, 5 or 10-year windows.
  • Asset class comparisons. Putting stocks, bonds, gold and real estate on the same footing over the same time horizon.
  • Goal planning. Working backwards from a target amount to find the growth rate, starting amount, or time horizon needed to reach it.

Limitations of CAGR

CAGR smooths reality into a single number, which is exactly what makes it useful — and exactly what it can hide. Two positions can share an identical CAGR while one grew in an almost straight line and the other lurched through a 60% drawdown along the way. CAGR also can't be extrapolated blindly: a high CAGR measured over a short, unusually strong window (say, three years coming out of a market bottom) is not a reliable predictor of the next ten years. Always check the underlying year-by-year path, and treat any historical CAGR as a description of the past rather than a promise about the future.

A worked example

Suppose an investment starts at $10,000 and grows to $25,937 after 10 years. Dividing 25,937 by 10,000 gives 2.5937; taking that to the power of 1/10 gives 1.10; subtracting 1 gives a CAGR of exactly 10%. Run the same $10,000 forward at that 10% CAGR for 15 years instead of 10, and it grows to roughly $41,772 — the extra five years compound on an already-larger base, which is why the "Ending Value" tab above shows the balance accelerating rather than growing in a straight line.

Nominal vs. real (inflation-adjusted) CAGR

The headline CAGR above is nominal — it doesn't subtract inflation eroding the purchasing power of that growth. The milestones section includes a real CAGR, calculated as (1 + nominal CAGR) ÷ (1 + inflation rate) − 1, using a default 3% inflation assumption you can adjust in the advanced assumptions panel.

Calculating CAGR for periods under a year

CAGR is always expressed on an annual basis, but the two dates you're comparing don't have to be a year apart — the "Time horizon" fields above accept days, weeks, months or quarters and convert them to a fractional-year count before running the same annualized formula. Annualizing a short period amplifies the rate: $10,000 growing to $11,000 over 8 months is a 10% total return, but expressed as an annual rate that's a 15.37% CAGR, because the calculation asks "if this pace continued for a full year, what rate would that be?" Treat any CAGR measured over less than a year as a projection, not a settled result — short windows are the most likely to be distorted by one-off swings.

Glossary of terms

Beginning Value
The value of the investment, portfolio or metric at the start of the period you're measuring.
Ending Value
The value at the end of the period — what the beginning value grew (or shrank) into.
CAGR
The single constant annual growth rate that explains the move from beginning value to ending value over the stated years.
Total / Absolute Return
The overall percentage change from beginning to ending value, with no regard for how many years it took.
Arithmetic Average Return
The simple average of each year's percentage return — tends to overstate real growth whenever returns are volatile.
XIRR / IRR
Money-weighted return measures that account for multiple cash flows at different dates, unlike CAGR's single lump-sum assumption.
Real vs. Nominal CAGR
Nominal is the raw percentage growth rate; real CAGR subtracts the effect of inflation to show growth in actual purchasing power.
Rule of 72
A shortcut estimate for years-to-double a lump sum: divide 72 by the CAGR.

Common mistakes & tips for using CAGR well

  • Confusing CAGR with the average annual return. They're rarely the same number once volatility is involved — see the side-by-side comparison further down the page.
  • Applying CAGR when money was added along the way. If you made contributions or withdrawals between the start and end dates, CAGR on the raw values will be misleading — use XIRR instead.
  • Cherry-picking a short, favorable window. A CAGR measured from a market bottom to a market top will look far better than the long-run reality — always check what period is being measured.
  • Assuming a historical CAGR predicts the future. Past growth rates, especially over short windows, are not a reliable forecast of what comes next.
  • Ignoring the path in between. Two investments with an identical CAGR can have completely different risk profiles — one steady, one a rollercoaster.
  • Forgetting fees, taxes and inflation. The headline CAGR is nominal and pre-tax — check the milestones section for real and after-tax figures.
