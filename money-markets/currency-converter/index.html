Understanding currency conversion

An exchange rate expresses how many units of one currency it takes to buy one unit of another. This converter fetches that rate live from the Frankfurter API, which publishes European Central Bank (ECB) reference rates — official, institutional data rather than an estimate.

Converted Amount = Amount × Exchange Rate

Converting $100 at a rate of 0.87 EUR per USD gives €87.00. The rate itself is simply a ratio — how many euros one dollar currently buys — multiplied straight through by however much you're converting.

Mid-market rate vs. what you'll actually pay

The rate shown here is the mid-market rate — the midpoint between the buy and sell prices in the wholesale currency market, the same rate banks and large institutions use when trading with each other. It's a useful, unbiased benchmark, but it is not the rate you'll typically receive as a consumer. Credit and debit card networks usually apply a rate within about 1% of the mid-market rate, but card issuers commonly add their own foreign transaction fee of roughly 1–3% on top. Bank wire transfers and currency exchange services often build in a markup of 2–4% directly into their quoted rate rather than listing it as a separate fee, and dynamic currency conversion (being offered the choice to pay in your home currency instead of the local one) is typically the worst option, often carrying a 3–8% markup. The gap between the mid-market rate and what you actually pay is where these providers make their margin.

Why the rate doesn't move every second

Unlike a live stock or crypto ticker, ECB reference rates are published once per working day, typically around 16:00 CET, based on a daily concentration procedure among central banks. That means the rate shown here stays fixed between those daily updates and doesn't reflect intraday market movements — for most everyday conversion purposes this is a reasonable, stable benchmark, but it's not a live trading feed.

Reading the rate direction correctly

An exchange rate is always relative to a specific base currency, and mixing up the direction is a common source of error. "1 USD = 0.87 EUR" and "1 EUR = 1.14 USD" describe the exact same market relationship from two different starting points — inverting the rate without also inverting which currency is the base produces a conversion that's roughly the wrong order of magnitude. Always check which currency the rate is quoted "per" before doing the math by hand.

How a currency's strength changes over time

When a currency strengthens against another, it takes fewer units of it to buy one unit of the other currency — the money buys more than it used to. When it weakens, the opposite happens. Comparing today's rate to a rate from a week, month, or year ago (as the Rate Trend tab does) shows this shift directly, though currency markets move for many overlapping reasons — interest rate differences, inflation, trade balances, and broader risk sentiment among them — that are well beyond the scope of a simple percentage change.

Glossary of terms

Exchange rate
How many units of one currency it takes to buy one unit of another.
Base currency
The currency being converted from — the one the rate is quoted "per unit of."
Mid-market rate
The midpoint between buy and sell prices in the wholesale currency market, with no markup added.
Foreign transaction fee
A percentage fee card issuers charge on top of the network exchange rate for purchases in a foreign currency.
Dynamic Currency Conversion (DCC)
Being offered the option to pay in your home currency at a foreign merchant, typically at a worse rate than paying in the local currency.
ECB reference rate
The official daily exchange rate published by the European Central Bank, the data source behind this converter.
Appreciation / depreciation
A currency strengthening (appreciating) or weakening (depreciating) in value relative to another currency.
Spread
The gap between the buy and sell price a provider quotes, part of how banks and exchanges earn revenue on conversions.

Common mistakes & tips for currency conversion

  • Confusing the rate direction. Mixing up which currency is the base leads to conversions off by roughly the inverse of the rate.
  • Assuming the mid-market rate is what you'll receive. Real-world transfers, cards, and exchanges almost always apply their own markup on top.
  • Accepting Dynamic Currency Conversion at checkout. Paying in your home currency abroad is typically the most expensive conversion option available.
  • Treating the rate as continuously live. ECB reference rates update once a working day, not every second — don't expect intraday ticks.
  • Not comparing providers before a large transfer. Markups vary significantly between banks, card networks, and dedicated transfer services.
  • Ignoring weekends and holidays. Rates don't update when the ECB is closed, so the "latest" rate may be a business day or more old.
