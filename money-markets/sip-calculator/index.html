Understanding SIP returns

A Systematic Investment Plan (SIP) is a method of investing a fixed amount into a mutual fund at regular intervals, most commonly monthly, instead of committing a lump sum all at once. It works like a recurring deposit: a fixed amount is automatically invested each period, buying fund units at whatever price prevails that month.

FV = P × [((1 + i)ⁿ − 1) ÷ i] × (1 + i)

P is the monthly investment, i is the monthly rate of return (annual rate ÷ 12 ÷ 100), and n is the total number of months. This is the future value of an annuity due — each installment is treated as invested at the start of its month, so every payment gets one extra month of compounding compared to a standard end-of-period annuity. In the default example on this page — $500 a month at 12% expected annual return for 15 years — the projected maturity value is about $252,288, of which $90,000 is money actually invested and roughly $162,288 is estimated returns.

Why the formula includes an extra (1 + i) term

Because SIP installments are debited and invested at the beginning of each month rather than the end, each one compounds for one additional period. Using the same $500/month, 12%, 15-year example: the standard annuity-due SIP formula gives about $252,288, while the "ordinary annuity" version without that extra factor gives about $249,790 — a difference of roughly $2,498 purely from the timing convention, compounding to a larger gap on bigger amounts or longer horizons.

Step-up SIP: growing your investment with your income

A step-up (or top-up) SIP automatically increases the monthly investment amount by a fixed percentage at set intervals, typically annually, so contributions rise alongside income over time. In the same base example, stepping the SIP up by 10% every year lifts the total invested from $90,000 to roughly $190,635 over 15 years, and the projected maturity value from about $252,288 to roughly $434,192 — a substantially larger corpus, funded mostly by investing more as income grows rather than by a higher assumed return.

SIP vs. lump sum: a common misconception

SIP is often framed as inherently "better" than lump-sum investing, but the math usually says otherwise if the lump sum is actually available. Investing the full amount immediately lets all of it compound from day one; a SIP spreads the same total out, so a large share of it only starts compounding much later in the period. SIP's real advantage isn't a higher expected return — it's practical and behavioral: it lets someone build a large position using income they don't have yet, on an automatic, disciplined schedule, without needing to time when to invest a lump sum.

Rupee-cost averaging

Investing the same fixed amount every month automatically buys more fund units when prices are low and fewer when prices are high, which averages the purchase cost over time. This doesn't guarantee a better outcome than a lump sum in a rising market, but it does reduce the specific risk of committing a large sum right before a downturn, and it removes the pressure to pick a single "right" entry point.

Finding the SIP needed for a specific goal

The same formula rearranges to solve for the monthly contribution needed to reach a target amount: PMT = Target FV ÷ {[((1+i)ⁿ−1) ÷ i] × (1+i)}. Solving for a $252,288 target at 12% over 15 years gives back exactly $500 a month, confirming the formula works consistently in both directions.

What this calculator doesn't do

It assumes a constant, smooth annual return every year, which no real mutual fund delivers — actual returns are volatile year to year even when long-run averages are strong. It doesn't subtract inflation, fund expense ratios, exit loads, or capital gains tax, all of which reduce the real, spendable value of the final corpus. Treat the output as a directional planning estimate, not a promise of what will actually happen.

Glossary of terms

SIP (Systematic Investment Plan)
A method of investing a fixed amount into a mutual fund at regular intervals, most commonly monthly.
Maturity value
The projected total value of a SIP investment at the end of the chosen investment period.
Annuity due
A series of equal payments made at the start of each period, giving each payment one extra period of compounding versus an ordinary annuity.
Step-up (top-up) SIP
A SIP where the monthly investment amount increases by a set percentage at regular intervals, typically annually.
Rupee-cost averaging
The effect of investing a fixed amount regularly, which buys more units when prices are low and fewer when prices are high, averaging the purchase cost over time.
Lump sum investing
Investing the full amount available immediately in a single transaction, rather than spreading it across multiple periods.
Expected annual return
The assumed constant yearly growth rate used to project a SIP's future value — an estimate, not a guarantee.
Wealth gain
The portion of a SIP's maturity value that comes from investment growth rather than money actually contributed.

Common mistakes & tips for SIP projections

  • Using an overly optimistic return rate. A few points too high compounds into a wildly overstated projection over long horizons.
  • Stopping contributions during a market downturn. This undermines rupee-cost averaging, which specifically depends on continuing to buy units when prices are low.
  • Ignoring inflation and taxes entirely. The nominal maturity value is not the real, spendable amount after prices rise and gains are taxed.
  • Assuming SIP always beats a lump sum. If the full amount is genuinely available today, investing it immediately usually produces a higher expected value than spreading it via SIP.
  • Never stepping up contributions. Keeping a fixed SIP amount for decades ignores rising income and leaves meaningful growth on the table compared to a step-up SIP.
  • Treating a single projection as a guarantee. Real mutual fund returns vary significantly year to year — use this as a planning tool to stress-test scenarios, not a forecast.

Annuity due vs. ordinary annuity — why the timing matters

$500/month, 12% annual return, 15 years (180 months) — the same inputs, two different payment-timing conventions.

ConventionPayment timingMaturity value
Ordinary annuityEnd of each month$249,790.10
Annuity due (SIP standard)Start of each month$252,288.00

The $2,498 gap comes purely from timing — every SIP installment under the annuity-due convention gets one extra month of compounding because it's invested immediately rather than at month-end. This is why SIP calculators universally use the annuity-due formula: it matches how SIP installments actually work, being debited and invested right at the start of the billing cycle.
