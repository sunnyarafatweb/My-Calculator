Understanding break-even analysis

The break-even point is the sales level at which total revenue exactly equals total costs — fixed plus variable — so the business reports neither a profit nor a loss. Every unit sold beyond that point contributes directly to profit; every unit short of it means the business is still operating at a loss.

Break-Even Units = Fixed Costs ÷ (Price per Unit − Variable Cost per Unit)

The denominator — price minus variable cost — is the contribution margin per unit: the portion of each sale left over after covering the direct cost of making or delivering that unit, available to pay down fixed costs and, once those are covered, become profit. In the default example on this page — $10,000 in fixed costs, a $35 price, and $15 variable cost per unit — the contribution margin is $20, giving a break-even point of 500 units, or $17,500 in revenue.

Fixed costs vs. variable costs

Fixed costs stay the same regardless of production volume — rent, salaried staff, and insurance are typical examples, incurred whether the business sells zero units or ten thousand. Variable costs scale directly with volume — raw materials, hourly labor, and shipping rise and fall with how much is actually produced and sold. Getting this classification right matters enormously, since misclassifying a cost throws off the entire break-even calculation. Semi-variable costs, which contain both a fixed base and a variable component (a phone plan with a flat fee plus per-minute charges, for example), are best split into their fixed and variable portions rather than lumped into one category.

Contribution margin ratio

Expressing contribution margin as a percentage of price gives the contribution margin ratio — (Price − Variable Cost) ÷ Price × 100 — useful for break-even analysis in revenue terms and for comparing efficiency across products with very different price points. Businesses with very low variable costs, like software or digital products, often have contribution margin ratios above 70–80%, letting them reach profitability at comparatively low sales volumes; businesses with thin per-unit margins, like grocery retail, need much higher volumes to cover the same fixed costs.

Target profit: break-even with a goal attached

The same formula extends naturally to answer a more useful question than "when do we merely break even" — how many units are needed to hit a specific profit target.

Target Profit Units = (Fixed Costs + Desired Profit) ÷ Contribution Margin per Unit

A business with $18,000 in fixed costs, a $100 price, and a $20 variable cost has an $80 contribution margin and a 225-unit break-even point; targeting $16,000 in profit instead raises that to 425 units — the same formula, simply covering a bigger number than zero.

Three levers that lower the break-even point

Starting from the default example (500 units to break even), each lever moves the number differently: cutting fixed costs by 20% (to $8,000) drops break-even to 400 units; cutting variable cost per unit by 20% (to $12) drops it to about 435 units; raising price by 10% (to $38.50) drops it to about 426 units. All three genuinely help, but fixed-cost cuts flow straight through without touching the contribution margin, while price and variable-cost changes work by widening that margin — the right lever (or combination) depends on which one is realistic for the specific business.

Selling multiple products: the sales mix

When a business sells more than one product, a single break-even number requires a weighted-average contribution margin based on each product's share of total sales.

Weighted Avg Contribution Margin = Σ(Contribution Margin per Product × Sales Mix %)

Total break-even units are then fixed costs divided by that weighted average, and can be split back out per product using the same sales mix percentages. If the actual mix shifts — selling proportionally more of a low-margin product than assumed — the true break-even point moves even if total unit volume looks unchanged.

Margin of safety

Margin of safety measures how far current or expected sales sit above the break-even point — Current Sales − Break-Even Sales, often expressed as a percentage of current sales. A business selling 700 units against a 500-unit break-even point has a 200-unit margin of safety, or roughly 29% — a rough cushion indicating how much sales could fall before the business dips back into a loss.

Glossary of terms

Break-even point
The sales level, in units or revenue, at which total revenue exactly equals total costs.
Fixed costs
Expenses that stay the same regardless of production or sales volume, such as rent or salaried staff.
Variable costs
Expenses that scale directly with production or sales volume, such as raw materials or hourly labor.
Contribution margin
The amount left from a unit's selling price after covering its variable cost, available to pay fixed costs and profit.
Contribution margin ratio
Contribution margin expressed as a percentage of the selling price.
Sales mix
The proportion each product represents of a business's total unit sales, used to weight a multi-product break-even analysis.
Margin of safety
The amount by which current or expected sales exceed the break-even point, showing a cushion against a downturn.
Semi-variable cost
A cost with both a fixed base component and a variable component that scales with volume.

Common mistakes & tips for break-even analysis

  • Misclassifying costs. Treating a cost that actually scales with volume as fixed, or vice versa, throws off the entire calculation.
  • Forgetting irregular fixed costs. A quarterly or annual expense needs converting to a consistent monthly figure to fit alongside truly monthly fixed costs.
  • Ignoring semi-variable costs. Lumping a partly-fixed, partly-variable cost entirely into one bucket distorts the contribution margin.
  • Using a single blended break-even for very different products. A shifting sales mix between high- and low-margin products moves the true break-even point even when total volume looks stable.
  • Treating break-even as a static, one-time number. Costs and prices change — revisit the calculation whenever fixed costs, pricing, or input costs shift meaningfully.
  • Forgetting margin of safety. Knowing the break-even point alone doesn't show how much cushion current sales actually provide against a downturn.
