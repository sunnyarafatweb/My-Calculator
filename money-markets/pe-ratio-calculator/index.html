Understanding the P/E ratio

The price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio is the most widely quoted valuation multiple in equity markets. It compresses two pieces of information — what the market is paying for a share, and what the company earns per share — into a single number that makes it possible to compare very differently priced stocks on a common basis.

P/E Ratio = Share Price ÷ Earnings Per Share (EPS)

In the default example on this page — a $200 stock with $5 of trailing EPS — the P/E ratio is 40, meaning investors are currently paying $40 for every $1 of the company's past annual earnings. The ratio is undefined for companies with zero or negative earnings, since there's no meaningful profit to divide the price by.

Trailing P/E vs. forward P/E

Trailing (or TTM, "trailing twelve months") P/E uses EPS actually reported over the past year — it's objective and grounded in confirmed results, but backward-looking. Forward P/E instead uses an analyst's estimated EPS for the next 12 months, which is more relevant for a company expected to grow quickly, but only as reliable as the estimate behind it. A fast-growing company often looks expensive on trailing P/E and considerably more reasonable on forward P/E, since the denominator (earnings) is expected to rise. Using both together — trailing for what's confirmed, forward for what's priced in — gives a fuller picture than either alone.

PEG ratio: adjusting P/E for growth

A P/E ratio on its own says nothing about how fast a company is growing, which is exactly what the PEG ratio adds.

PEG Ratio = P/E Ratio ÷ EPS Growth Rate

The growth rate is entered as a whole number matching its percentage — 30 for 30% growth, not 0.30. A company trading at a P/E of 40 with 30% expected earnings growth has a PEG of about 1.33, generally considered reasonable for a fast-growing business, even though the P/E alone might look expensive at first glance. A PEG near 1.0 is often treated as a rough fair-value benchmark: below 1.0 can suggest the stock is undervalued relative to its growth, and above 2.0 can suggest it's overvalued relative to its growth — though PEG works best for mature, profitable companies with fairly stable growth rates, and is less reliable for volatile or unprofitable ones.

Earnings yield: flipping the ratio around

Earnings yield is simply the inverse of P/E — EPS divided by price, expressed as a percentage. A P/E of 20 corresponds to a 5% earnings yield, a P/E of 40 to a 2.5% earnings yield. Expressing valuation this way makes it easier to compare a stock's earnings-based return to a bond yield or another fixed-income alternative on directly comparable percentage terms.

Why sector context matters more than an absolute number

Technology and other high-growth sectors have historically traded at meaningfully higher P/E multiples than sectors like utilities, energy, or financials — reflecting differences in growth expectations, capital intensity, and margin profiles rather than one being systematically over- or under-valued. As a rough, time-varying reference: broad technology and consumer discretionary sectors have recently traded near 30x forward earnings, while energy and financials have traded closer to 15–16x, with the S&P 500 as a whole around 21x. Comparing a stock's P/E only to the market average, rather than its direct sector peers, can produce a misleading sense of whether it's cheap or expensive.

Implied fair value: working the formula backwards

The same formula rearranges to estimate what price a given multiple would justify: Implied Price = Target P/E × EPS. Applying an 18x industry-average multiple to $5 of EPS implies a $90 fair value; if the stock is actually trading at $75, that's roughly 20% below the industry-implied level — a potential signal worth investigating further, not a guarantee the market is wrong. The target multiple chosen (industry average, historical average, or a personal estimate) drives the entire result, so the conclusion is only as good as that assumption.

What P/E doesn't capture

P/E ignores debt and capital structure entirely, so two companies with identical P/E ratios can carry very different financial risk. It can also be distorted by one-off items — write-downs, asset sales, tax credits — that temporarily inflate or depress reported earnings without reflecting the ongoing business. P/E is best used alongside other measures like EV/EBITDA, free cash flow yield, and the PEG ratio, rather than as a standalone verdict on value.

Glossary of terms

P/E ratio
Share price divided by earnings per share, showing how much investors pay for each dollar of annual profit.
EPS (Earnings Per Share)
A company's net income divided by its total shares outstanding.
Trailing P/E (TTM)
P/E calculated using actual earnings reported over the past 12 months.
Forward P/E
P/E calculated using an analyst's estimated earnings for the next 12 months.
PEG ratio
The P/E ratio divided by the expected annual earnings growth rate, adjusting valuation for growth.
Earnings yield
The inverse of the P/E ratio — EPS divided by price — expressed as a percentage.
Implied fair value
The share price a target P/E multiple would justify, given current EPS.
Value trap
A stock that looks cheap on a low P/E but is actually facing declining earnings or structural business problems.

Common mistakes & tips for using P/E ratio

  • Comparing P/E across unrelated sectors. A software company and a utility trade at structurally different multiples for reasons that have nothing to do with which one is "cheaper."
  • Treating a low P/E as an automatic bargain. A depressed multiple can reflect genuine undervaluation, or it can reflect declining earnings and real business problems — the ratio alone can't tell you which.
  • Ignoring one-off items in earnings. A write-down, asset sale, or tax credit can distort EPS for a single period, making that period's P/E misleading.
  • Using P/E on unprofitable companies. With zero or negative earnings, the ratio is undefined or meaningless — use a different multiple instead.
  • Skipping growth context. A high P/E can be entirely reasonable for a fast-growing company; check the PEG ratio before assuming a high multiple means overvalued.
  • Relying on P/E in isolation. Pair it with debt levels, free cash flow, and sector comparisons rather than treating it as a complete valuation on its own.

Same P/E, different story — why growth context matters

Two companies, both trading at a P/E of 20, with very different expected earnings growth.

CompanyP/EExpected growthPEG
Company A20x10%/yr2.00
Company B20x25%/yr0.80

Both companies look identically valued on P/E alone, but Company B's much faster expected growth means investors are paying far less per unit of future growth — a PEG of 0.80 versus 2.00. This is exactly the blind spot the PEG ratio is designed to fix: two stocks can share the same multiple while representing very different value once growth is taken into account.
