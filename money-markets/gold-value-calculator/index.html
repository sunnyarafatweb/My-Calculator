Understanding gold value

Gold's value is determined by three things: the current spot price of pure gold, how much an item weighs, and how pure that gold actually is. This calculator fetches the spot price live from Gold-API.com, a free precious metals price service, so the number is based on today's real market — not an estimate you have to look up yourself.

Melt Value = (Spot Price per Troy Ounce ÷ 31.1035) × Weight in Grams × (Karat ÷ 24)

The spot price is quoted per troy ounce, so dividing by 31.1035 (the number of grams in a troy ounce) converts it to a price per gram of pure, 24K gold. Multiplying by the item's weight and then by its purity fraction — karat divided by 24 — gives the value of the actual gold content. A 10-gram 14K item, for instance, contains the gold-equivalent of 10 × (14 ÷ 24) ≈ 5.83 grams of pure gold.

Karat and purity, explained

Karat measures how many of 24 parts of an alloy are pure gold — 24K is 99.9% pure, 18K is 18 ⁄ 24 or exactly 75%, and 14K is 14 ⁄ 24, about 58.3%. Pure 24K gold is soft and prone to scratching and bending, so most jewelry uses a lower karat alloyed with metals like copper, silver, or zinc for strength and color. Many pieces also carry a millesimal fineness stamp instead of or alongside the karat mark — 999 for 24K, 916 for 22K, 750 for 18K, 585 for 14K, and 417 for 10K — expressing the same purity as parts per thousand rather than parts per 24.

Weight units: grams, troy ounces, and tola

Precious metals are conventionally priced per troy ounce (about 31.1035 grams), which is roughly 10% heavier than the everyday avoirdupois ounce used for groceries and shipping — mixing the two up is a common source of calculation errors. In South Asia and parts of the Middle East, gold is traditionally weighed in tola, a unit equal to about 11.6638 grams, still widely used in jewelry pricing and trading across the region.

Melt value vs. what a dealer will actually pay

Melt value is the theoretical worth of the gold content alone, calculated directly from weight, purity, and spot price. It is not the same as what a pawnbroker, jeweler, or scrap gold buyer will actually offer. Dealers need to cover refining costs, testing, transport, and their own margin, so real-world payouts commonly land somewhere in a rough 70–85% range of melt value — sometimes lower for small quantities or from less competitive buyers. Getting quotes from multiple buyers is the most reliable way to find out what a specific item is actually worth to sell.

What melt value doesn't capture

A pure weight-and-purity calculation says nothing about a piece's other sources of value. Designer jewelry, branded pieces, antiques, and certain collectible coins can carry meaningful premiums above their metal content alone. Conversely, hollow construction, gemstone settings (whose weight shouldn't be counted as gold), and gold-plated rather than solid items can all mean the true gold content — and therefore the true value — is lower than a naive calculation based on total item weight would suggest.

Silver follows the same math

Silver jewelry, coins, and bullion are valued with an identical approach — weight × purity × today's per-gram price of pure silver — just using silver's spot price and its own standard purities: 999 (fine silver), 925 (sterling silver), and 900 (coin silver) are the most common.

Glossary of terms

Spot price
The current market price for immediate delivery of a precious metal, quoted per troy ounce.
Karat (K)
A measure of gold purity out of 24 parts — 24K is pure gold, 18K is 75% gold, and so on.
Millesimal fineness
Purity expressed in parts per thousand instead of karat, such as 999, 916, 750, or 585.
Melt value
The value of an item's precious metal content alone, based on weight, purity, and spot price.
Troy ounce
The standard unit for precious metals, equal to about 31.1035 grams — different from a regular ounce.
Tola
A traditional South Asian and Middle Eastern unit of mass for gold, equal to about 11.6638 grams.
Sterling silver
An alloy that is 92.5% pure silver, marked with a "925" stamp.
Scrap gold
Gold jewelry or items sold for their metal content rather than as intact, wearable pieces.

Common mistakes & tips for valuing gold

  • Confusing troy ounces with regular ounces. A troy ounce is about 10% heavier — using the wrong conversion understates the value.
  • Assuming melt value is the final sale price. Real dealer payouts are typically below melt value to cover their costs and margin.
  • Not deducting gemstone or non-gold weight. A ring's total weight includes any stones, clasps, or fillers, which shouldn't be counted as gold.
  • Trusting an unmarked item's assumed purity. Without a visible hallmark or professional testing, purity can't be confirmed with confidence.
  • Ignoring premium value for collectible or designer pieces. Some items are genuinely worth more than their metal content alone.
  • Getting only one dealer quote. Payout percentages vary meaningfully between buyers — compare more than one before selling.
