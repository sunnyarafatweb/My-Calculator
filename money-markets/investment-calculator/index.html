Understanding investment growth

An investment calculator projects how a starting balance, combined with regular contributions, grows over time at an assumed rate of return. The math has two moving parts: your initial lump sum compounds on its own, and each new contribution starts compounding from the moment it's added, so contributions made early in your timeline have far more time to grow than contributions made near the end.

Future Value = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t) + PMT × [((1 + r/n)^(n×t) − 1) ÷ (r/n)]

P is your initial investment, PMT is your periodic contribution, r is the annual return rate, n is how many times per year returns compound, and t is years. The first term grows your starting balance on its own; the second term grows the stream of contributions you add along the way. In the default example on this page — $10,000 initial, $500 a month, 7% annual return, 25 years, monthly compounding — the projected future value is about $462,290, of which $160,000 is money you actually contributed and roughly $302,290 is investment growth.

What return rate is realistic?

The S&P 500 has returned close to 10% annually on average since 1928, including reinvested dividends — but that's a nominal figure. After adjusting for inflation, the real historical return has been closer to 7%. Many financial planners use a more conservative 6–8% for diversified stock portfolios and 3–5% for bond-heavy allocations, since any given decade can land well above or below the long-run average, and past performance never guarantees future results.

Why compounding frequency matters less than people expect

At typical stock-market return rates, the difference between monthly and daily compounding is small — a few tenths of a percent in the effective annual rate. The much bigger levers are your contribution amount, your time horizon, and the return rate itself. Compounding frequency matters more for products like high-yield savings accounts or CDs with fixed, published rates, where the exact frequency is stated in the terms.

Nominal value vs. real (inflation-adjusted) value

A dollar amount decades from now isn't worth the same as a dollar today, because prices rise over that time too. Dividing the nominal future value by (1 + inflation rate) raised to the number of years shows the real, inflation-adjusted value — what that future balance would be able to buy in today's terms. In the default example, a $462,290 nominal balance in 25 years is worth roughly $220,792 in today's purchasing power at a 3% assumed inflation rate — a significant, easy-to-overlook difference that matters for realistic goal-setting.

Solving backwards: required contribution and time to goal

The same formula can be rearranged to answer two other common questions. Given a target future value, initial investment, and return rate, the required monthly contribution is found by solving the formula for PMT directly. Given a target future value, initial investment, contribution and return rate, the time needed is found numerically — the equation can't be rearranged into a simple closed-form solution when both a lump sum and a contribution stream are involved, so this calculator searches for the number of years where the projected value matches your goal.

How much of the final number is really "yours"?

In the default example, about 65% of the final $462,290 balance comes from investment growth rather than money actually contributed — compounding is doing most of the work by year 25, even though the growth "invisible" in any single year. This is the core case for starting early: a dollar contributed in year one has decades to compound, while a dollar contributed in the final year barely grows at all before the time horizon ends.

What this calculator doesn't do

It assumes a constant, smooth rate of return every year, which no real investment delivers — markets rise and fall unevenly even when the long-run average holds up. It doesn't account for taxes, investment fees, or contribution timing within each period (it assumes contributions land at the end of each compounding period). Treat the output as a planning estimate to stress-test different scenarios, not a promise of what will actually happen.

Glossary of terms

Future value (FV)
What an investment is projected to be worth at a future date, given a starting balance, contributions, and a rate of return.
Principal
Your initial lump-sum investment, before any contributions or growth are added.
Compounding frequency
How often investment returns are credited and start earning further returns — daily, monthly, quarterly or annually.
Nominal value
A future dollar amount expressed in the dollars of that future year, without adjusting for inflation.
Real value
A future dollar amount adjusted for inflation, expressed in today's purchasing power.
Rate of return
The annual percentage an investment is assumed to grow by, before fees and taxes.
Time horizon
The number of years between now and when the invested funds are needed.
Rule of 72
A quick estimate for how many years it takes an investment to double: 72 divided by the annual return rate.

Common mistakes & tips for investment projections

  • Using an overly optimistic return rate. A rate a few points too high compounds into a wildly overstated projection over multi-decade horizons.
  • Ignoring inflation entirely. A large nominal number decades from now can represent meaningfully less real purchasing power than it appears to.
  • Assuming a smooth, constant return every year. Real markets are volatile even when long-run averages are strong — a projection is a planning tool, not a forecast of any single year.
  • Forgetting fees and taxes. Fund expense ratios and taxes on gains both reduce your real-world return below the headline rate used in a projection.
  • Delaying contributions while waiting for a "better" market. Because early contributions have the most time to compound, delaying often costs more than a modestly worse entry price.
  • Not revisiting the plan. A projection made once and never updated drifts further from reality every year — recheck it periodically against actual results.

Nominal vs. real value — why the difference matters

$10,000 initial investment, $500/month, 7% annual return, 25 years, monthly compounding, 3% assumed inflation.

MeasureValueWhat it means
Nominal future value$462,290.03The projected balance in future dollars
Real (inflation-adjusted) value$220,792.29What that balance buys in today's purchasing power

The nominal figure is what will actually be in the account; the real figure is what that amount is worth once 25 years of assumed 3% inflation are stripped back out. Both numbers are "correct" — they just answer different questions. Planning around the nominal figure alone risks understating how much you'll actually need to save to maintain a given standard of living decades from now.
