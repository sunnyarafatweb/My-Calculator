Understanding average stock price

When you buy the same stock at different times and prices, your average cost per share isn't a simple average of those prices — it's a weighted average, where larger purchases count for more than smaller ones. This weighted average is also called your cost basis, and it doubles as your break-even price: the level the stock needs to reach for you to come out even before fees or taxes.

Average Price = (Price₁ × Shares₁ + Price₂ × Shares₂ + …) ÷ Total Shares

In the default example on this page — 100 shares bought at $50, then 100 more at $40 — the average works out to ($5,000 + $4,000) ÷ 200 = $45.00 per share. With the stock now trading at $52, that's an unrealized gain of $7 per share, or $1,400 across the 200-share position.

Why a weighted average, not a simple one

A simple average of two prices treats them as equally important regardless of how much was actually invested at each. Buying 200 shares at $50 and 100 shares at $80 has a simple average of $65, but a weighted average of only $60 — because the larger, cheaper purchase pulls the true cost basis down more than an equal-weighted calculation would suggest. The weighted average is what actually determines your break-even point and your real profit or loss; the simple average of the prices you paid is not a meaningful number for that purpose.

Averaging down: what it does and doesn't do

Averaging down means buying more shares of a position you already hold after its price has fallen, which lowers your weighted average cost and therefore your break-even price. If you hold 100 shares at a $75 average and the stock falls to $50, buying $5,000 more at that price (100 more shares) brings your average down to $62.50 — the price only needs to recover to $62.50 for you to break even, instead of all the way back to $75. But averaging down doesn't change why the price fell in the first place; it only pays off if the stock genuinely recovers. If it keeps falling, you've simply committed more capital to a position that's still losing money.

Averaging down vs. dollar-cost averaging

These are often confused but conceptually different. Dollar-cost averaging is a systematic, scheduled strategy — investing a fixed amount at regular intervals regardless of what the price is doing, which naturally buys more shares when prices are low and fewer when they're high. Averaging down is a discretionary, reactive decision to add specifically because a price has dropped, typically evaluated case by case rather than on a fixed schedule.

Fees change your real cost basis

A purchase commission or transaction fee adds directly to your cost basis. Buying 100 shares at $50 with a $10 commission means your true cost is $50.10 per share ($5,010 ÷ 100), not the $50.00 sticker price. Small per-trade fees add up across multiple purchases, and ignoring them slightly understates your real break-even point.

How the IRS treats cost basis for stocks

Per IRS Publication 550, the average cost method is specifically available for mutual fund shares and stock enrolled in a dividend reinvestment plan (DRIP), and must be formally elected with the broker or fund company. For most individual stock purchases, the default method for tax reporting is FIFO (first-in, first-out) unless you specifically identify which lot you're selling at the time of sale. The weighted average this calculator computes is a useful personal tracking number regardless — for knowing your break-even and performance — separate from whichever method applies for your actual tax reporting.

Finding the shares needed to hit a target average

The same formula rearranges to answer a common practical question: how many more shares, bought at a specific price, would bring the average down (or up) to an exact target? Solving 100 shares at a $75 average for the share count needed at a $50 buy price to reach a $60 target average gives exactly 150 additional shares — verified by plugging back in: (100 × 75 + 150 × 50) ÷ 250 = $60.00 exactly.

Glossary of terms

Average price (cost basis)
The weighted average price paid per share across all purchases of a position.
Break-even price
The price a position needs to reach to neither gain nor lose money — the same number as the average price.
Weighted average
An average that accounts for the size of each purchase, so larger transactions influence the result more than smaller ones.
Averaging down
Buying more shares of a position after its price has fallen, to lower the overall average cost.
Averaging up
Buying more shares of a position that has risen in price, which raises the overall average cost.
Unrealized profit/loss
The paper gain or loss on a position based on the current market price, before it's actually sold.
Dollar-cost averaging (DCA)
Investing a fixed amount on a regular schedule regardless of price, as distinct from the reactive strategy of averaging down.
FIFO (first-in, first-out)
The default tax accounting method for most individual stock sales, treating the oldest shares as the ones sold first.

Common mistakes & tips for tracking average price

  • Using a simple average instead of a weighted one. Averaging your purchase prices directly, ignoring share counts, gives the wrong break-even number whenever purchase sizes differ.
  • Forgetting fees and commissions. Small per-trade costs add up and shift your true cost basis, even if only slightly.
  • Averaging down without revisiting the original thesis. A falling price alone isn't a reason to buy more — check whether the reason you bought in the first place still holds.
  • Confusing averaging down with dollar-cost averaging. One is a scheduled, systematic plan; the other is a reactive decision to add to a specific losing position.
  • Assuming the average cost tax method applies automatically. For most individual stocks, FIFO is the default unless you specifically elect and confirm another method with your broker.
  • Treating unrealized gains as locked in. A paper profit can disappear before you sell — it isn't realized until the position is actually closed.

Weighted average vs. simple average — why it matters

Two purchases of the same stock: 200 shares at $50, then 100 shares at $80.

MethodCalculationResult
Simple average (wrong for this purpose)($50 + $80) ÷ 2$65.00
Weighted average (your real cost basis)(200×$50 + 100×$80) ÷ 300$60.00

The simple average ignores that twice as many shares were bought at the lower price, overstating the true break-even by $5 per share. The weighted average is the number that actually reflects how much was invested and at what effective cost — always use it for break-even and profit/loss tracking, never a plain average of the prices paid.
