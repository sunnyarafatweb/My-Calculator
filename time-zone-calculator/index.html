How This Calculator Works

Choose a city to convert from, a date, and a time, then choose the city you want the equivalent time in. The result updates instantly and shows the local time, the calendar date, and whether that date is the same, the next, or the previous day relative to the one you entered. The world clock and time-zone math below run entirely in your browser using its built-in time zone database — the same source operating systems and calendar apps rely on — so daylight saving is applied correctly for the exact date you choose, not just for today.

Time Zones, UTC, and How the Math Works

Every time zone is defined as an offset from Coordinated Universal Time (UTC), the single global reference clock that never changes for daylight saving. A city's local time is simply UTC + that city's current offset. To convert a time from one city to another, this calculator first works out the exact UTC instant your entered time corresponds to in the "from" city (using that city's offset on that specific date), then reapplies the "to" city's offset for that same instant to get the converted local time.

The reason this has to be done in two steps rather than a single fixed subtraction is daylight saving time (DST). Most of the United States, Canada, Europe, and a handful of other regions shift their clocks forward by an hour for part of the year and back again later — which means the gap between, say, New York and London is 5 hours in January but only 4 hours in July, since the two regions don't always start and end DST on the same dates. Cities like Dhaka, Mumbai, Tokyo, and Singapore never observe daylight saving at all, so their offset from UTC is fixed year-round.

Time Zone Abbreviations vs. UTC Offsets

Three-letter abbreviations like EST, PST, or IST are common in everyday conversation, but they're a less precise way to describe a time zone than either a city name or a direct UTC offset. An abbreviation typically only pins down one side of a daylight-saving pair (EST for winter, EDT for summer, in the US Eastern zone, for instance), and as covered below, several abbreviations are reused by entirely different parts of the world. A UTC offset like UTC−5 is unambiguous but still changes with the seasons in DST-observing regions, which is exactly why this calculator asks for a city rather than a raw offset — the city carries both pieces of information (the base offset and whether DST applies) automatically for whatever date you pick.

Common Time Zone Mistakes (and What This Doesn't Cover)

The single most common mistake is treating a time zone abbreviation as if it only meant one thing worldwide. CST is US Central Standard Time to most Americans, but it's also the standard abbreviation for China Standard Time, and IST could mean India, Israel, or Ireland depending on who's speaking. When in doubt, use a city name or a full UTC offset instead of an abbreviation — which is the approach this calculator takes throughout.

A second gap worth knowing about: the one hour each spring and fall when a region's clocks physically move is genuinely ambiguous or has a "missing" hour (in the fall, 1:30 AM can occur twice; in the spring, 2:30 AM never occurs at all). Every calendar system, this one included, has to make a judgment call for times that fall inside that hour-long window, so if you're converting a time right at a DST changeover date, double-check the result against an official source before relying on it for something time-critical like a flight or a legal deadline.

Finally, a few countries deliberately use non-hour offsets — India and Sri Lanka sit at UTC+5:30, Iran at UTC+3:30, and Nepal at an unusual UTC+5:45 — so "the difference is always a whole number of hours" is an assumption that doesn't hold globally, even though it's true for the large majority of city pairs.

Scheduling Meetings Across Time Zones

For any two cities, the easiest approach is to find the window where normal business hours — roughly 9 AM to 5 PM — overlap in both places, which the timeline bars in the result above are built to show at a glance. A meeting between New York and London has a wide overlap (late morning through early afternoon Eastern Time works for both); a meeting between the US West Coast and Tokyo or Sydney often has little to no true business-hours overlap at all.

When a team spans regions with little overlap, two habits tend to work well in practice: rotate who takes the early-morning or late-night call from meeting to meeting rather than always asking the same office to accommodate everyone else, and record important calls so participants on the least convenient side of the clock aren't forced to choose between sleep and attendance every single time.

A Brief History of Standardized Time Zones

Before the late 1800s, most towns set their clocks to true local solar noon, which meant two places even a short distance apart could disagree by several minutes — a minor inconvenience on foot, but a serious scheduling problem once railways made it possible to travel city to city in hours rather than days. Railroad companies in North America began coordinating a small number of standardized "railway time" zones in 1883 to solve exactly that problem. The following year, delegates at the 1884 International Meridian Conference in Washington, D.C. adopted the Greenwich Observatory in England as the world's zero-degree reference line, giving rise to Greenwich Mean Time (GMT) and the 24-zone system — later refined into the atomic-clock-based UTC standard — that the whole world still builds its calendars around today.

Frequently Asked Questions

What time zone should I use if a city isn't in your list?

Pick the nearest major city that shares the same UTC offset and the same daylight saving rules — most neighboring cities within a country use identical time zone rules. If you're unsure, the UTC option lets you enter a known offset directly.

Does this calculator account for daylight saving time?

Yes, automatically. It uses your browser's built-in time zone database to look up the correct offset for each city on the specific date you enter, since many regions shift by an hour for part of the year while others (like Dhaka, Tokyo, or Mumbai) never observe daylight saving at all.

Why do some time zones have a 30 or 45-minute offset instead of a full hour?

A handful of countries deliberately set their standard time on a half-hour or even 45-minute line rather than the nearest full hour — India and Sri Lanka use UTC+5:30, Iran uses UTC+3:30, and Nepal uses UTC+5:45, usually for historical, geographic, or political reasons rather than anything mathematical.

What does the day-shift label like "+1 day" mean in my result?

It tells you whether the converted time lands on the same calendar day, the next day, or the previous day compared to the date you entered. Crossing several time zones at once can easily push a time past midnight in one direction, which matters when you're booking travel or scheduling a call.

Is "CST" always Central Standard Time?

No — CST is also widely used for China Standard Time, and IST commonly refers to India Standard Time, Israel Standard Time, or Irish Standard Time depending on context. Whenever an abbreviation could be ambiguous, confirm the country or use the city name instead, which is exactly why this calculator asks for cities rather than abbreviations.

What's the best time to schedule a meeting across many time zones?

Look for the window where normal business hours (roughly 9 AM to 5 PM) overlap for the most participants. When a group spans somewhere like the US and Asia with little or no overlap, it's common practice to rotate who takes the early or late call so the inconvenience doesn't always fall on the same team.

This calculator relies on your browser's time zone database and reflects each region's current daylight saving rules. Confirm the exact local time from an official source before relying on this for travel, legal, or other time-critical purposes — especially right around a daylight saving changeover date.
